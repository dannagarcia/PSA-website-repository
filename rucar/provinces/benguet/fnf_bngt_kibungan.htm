History and Government

In 1800, Kibungan was a virgin forest and sparsely populated area. During those days, the most feared people were the so-called “busol” or head hunters. As a result of this head hunting activity, some people of Mankayan, Buguias and Bakun moved southward and settled in the place. Sometime later, an American writer who was fond of adventure went to visit and observe the customs and traditions of the inhabitants. While he was there, he often heard the beautiful sounds of the gongs echoing on the towering rocky mountainsides during cañaos. To him, the sounds gave “kibong-kibong” rhythm. Hence, from the “kibong-kibong” sounds that were frequently heard, Kibungan's name was coined.

However, some old folks claim that it was derived from a species of monkeys called by the natives “kibong” that inhabited the virgin forest several years ago.

Hunting was a major activity during the early days where hunters who reached Kibungan established their home there. Most people came in and the population of the place grew.

Its Land

Kibungan is bounded by the municipality of Bakun on the north, and the east by Buguias, on the west by the province of Ilocos Sur , and on the south by the municipalities of Kapangan and Atok.

Kibungan has a total land are of 26,108.3799 hectares: 4,796.3123 has. are classified as alienable and disposable lands while the remaining 21,312.0676 has. are within the so-called unclassified public forest/timberland.

Among the barangays, Tacadang has the largest land area with 7,235.2121 has. while Sagpat is the smallest with 1,949.0487 has.

Benguet Pine trees dominate the forested zones. However, forest resources are practically diminishing. Forest destruction has been mainly caused by the slash-and -burn/swidden farming, forest fires, shifting cultivation and soil erosion.

Kibungan is popularly known in the province as the “Switzerland of Benguet” because of its alps and rocky mountains. Deep ravines and cliffs separate and isolate many sitios of some barangays. Although some plateaus, hills, and small valleys can be seen in the locality, Kibungan is dominantly mountainous in nature.

Aside from its beautiful and century old rice terraces, Kibungan has many rivers and streams. Waterfalls on high mountain cliffs decorate the mountains especially on rainy days. Many streams contribute to the formation of three big rivers that join the Amburayan River that eventually drains to the China Sea . The streams and rivers are rich with shrimps, eels, and other fishes that can thrive only on the mountains. Hot springs are also found at the foot of the mountains.

The mountain is within cool highland mountainous zone with elevations not more than 2,500 meters above sea level. Generally, the slope is more than 18%. During its coolest months of December and January, barangay Madaymen experiences chilling temperature of 0° Centigrade causing the famous “Snow of Madaymen.” The wet season is experienced from June to October and the dry months are from November to May.

Its Economy

Agriculture plays a dominant role in the economy of the municipality. The physical area planted to agricultural crops is about 15,559.1773 has. Only 35%, however, is irrigated while the remaining 65% depends on rain for irrigation.

Large proportions of the municipal land area are utilized for agricultural purposes. Originally, rice productions is Kibungan's main agricultural activity. However, with the advancement of agricultural technology, the largest portion of agricultural land, 26,108.3799 has. or 15.47% is being planted to rice while land devoted to vegetables is estimated to be at 5,403.8099 has. or 20.69%. Another major crop of the municipality is camote that is taking an area of 5,997.3781 has. or 22.97%. The remaining land area is planted with other crops.

Kibungan's rich natural resources have created various kinds of cottage industries such as gift and toy making, loom weaving, food processing, broom making, basket weaving, knitting, furniture making and garments.

These industries are mostly local-based, making use of locally produced materials and providing employment to individual entrepreneurs and organized groups particularly the women.
